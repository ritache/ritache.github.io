CaseWare Veracity

A cloud-based app for audit workflow management.

Role

UX/UI Designer

Timeline

6 months

Project type

Full-time

CaseWare Veracity is a cloud web application focused on providing internal auditors with the ability to plan, manage, and execute their audits all in one place. My team was eager to get the application into the hands of users and validate the hypotheses put forward by our product team and subject matter experts.

My role was to design the application from the ground up, introduce the team to the design thinking methodology, and validate Veracity with our target users.

01. Research

Prior to onboarding me as their designer, the Veracity team had done preliminary market research to discover that most small internal audit shops really struggled to find a solution that was easy to follow and set up.

Our company participated in a number of audit conferences, which was a perfect time to do some quantitative research in the form of surveys to hone down on what we assumed were some common pain points. The survey consisted of audit teams ranging anywhere from 5 to 50+ people.

Survey Findings

60%

keep track of risks and controls manually via spreadsheets.

60%

consider the large amount of manual effort as their primary pain point when auditing.

50%

use microsoft office products as their primary audit tool.

"We are using lots of tools that don’t talk to each other. We need a single source of truth."

Interview Findings

Key Takeaways

Remote work makes easy virtual communication even more critical.

Auditors often rely on tools that are not tailored to their work.

Showing audit’s value-add to a company is crucial.

Having smoother ways to transfer knowledge would make onboarding new staff much easier.

Pain Points

Over-complicated and expensive solutions.

Scattered audit files.

Friction when collaborating with clients.

Manual processes.

Opportunities

Audit teams using our platform need to be able to see what work has been done, which tasks they have on their plate, and shared access to files related to their projects. Our plan for Veracity was to encourage team collaboration via being able to assign tasks, tag users, and cross-reference any relevant objects within a project.

To address the issue of cumbersome and difficult-to-parse solutions, I made sure to focus on creating a guided, cohesive workflow, adding guidance, tooltips, and ensuring that each page provided enough context to the user with clear labeling and navigation. Keeping it simple and avoiding feature creep was key.

Research Synthesis

Our target customers are small and mid-sized audit teams, and synthesizing the user research resulted in 5 key user types. For our first release, we decided to focus on the lead and staff auditors as they would be using the application the most.

02. Design

Through interviews with end users and subject matter experts, our team outlined the feature roadmap and user stories for the first release of Veracity:

  • Overview dashboard
  • List of projects and their details
  • Core internal audit workflow
  • Audit findings and related action plans

I wanted to first address the pain points of complexity and lack of structure that auditors currently encounter, as that would give us the opportunity to work on the core audit workflow and build up to more complex features like automation and client interaction within Veracity in future releases.

Sketches

I created multiple versions of the various sections of the app, bringing in both product and development teams to review and provide their recommendations. Thanks to the technical and business input, we were able to refine terminology, front-end components, and object architecture of Veracity.

Refining the UI

Some areas of the app, such as dashboards, were already built prior to my joining the team. Since there were many upcoming critical features, we decided to not touch the functionality and focus on updating the UI to be more consistent with CaseWare’s brand guidelines.

Based on the research I conducted alongside our product team, there is a lot of opportunity for having the dashboards be tailored to the everyday work of a staff auditor, potentially showing assigned tasks or other notifications. The dashboard revisit went on our to-do list after some of the more critical functionality.

03. Testing

After doing internal testing of my high fidelity prototype, I was ready to put it in the hands of our early access customers. There were 5 participants in total, ranging from small to medium-sized audit shops. I wanted users to look at the app holistically to help them visualize how they would use it for the entire lifecycle of their projects.

Initial Prototype

Usability Testing

Users were asked to perform the following general tasks:

  • Navigating from the dashboard
  • Reviewing project list and details
  • Performing core audit work
  • Recording and tracking issues

These tasks also contained questions related to process and content; by nature of auditors not having a structured approach, different teams have quite different ways of performing similar functions. It’s key for Veracity to be generic and flexible enough to accommodate or improve on existing workflows.

"Just love the way it’s organized. Blown away by this."

All of the participants commented on the friendliness of the UI and how simple it was to find their way through the application. Two of the participants also mentioned that the structured layout of tasks in the project would make training new staff incredibly simple.

I also managed to gather a lot of suggestions on process, bringing in our product team to refine user stories and prioritize suggestions. One key area was control testing; our app performed automatic helper steps which clashed with the workflow of some of the auditors we interviewed.

Closing Thoughts

Working on a product from its very inception is an amazing experience. It was also an important lesson on asking the right questions and noting down assumptions. Relying on internal subject matter experts is a great way to get a solid foundation and learn what we don’t know, but it’s incomparable to being able to speak to real users.

Some of the improvements my team has made since the usability test:

Content

Adjusted filters and attributes of projects to improve project tracking.

Information architecture

Added the ability to reference files, tasks, and other objects to make it easier for auditors to gather evidence in one place.

Visuals

Refined dashboard layout for responsiveness and made date filtering more robust.
